## Chapter 6 Martingales

### 6.1 Martingales

**Definition** A stochastic process $(Z_n)$ is a **martingale** if $Z_n\in L^1\forall n$ and $E[Z_{n+1}\mid Z_1,...,Z_n]=Z_n$.

_Origin_ A martingale is a generalized version of a fair game: Interpret $Z_n$ as the gambler's fortune after $n$th game, then the mean fortune after $n+1$st game is equal to that after $n$th game, regardless of previous fortunes.

Taking expectations, we have $EZ_n\equiv EZ_1$.

**Examples** (1) Pick $X_i$ independent with mean $0$. Let $Z_n=\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$, then $Z_n$ is a martingale.

(2) Pick $X_i$ independent with mean $1$. Let $Z_n=\prod_{i=1}^nX_i$, then $Z_n$ is a martingale.

(3) Let $X_n$ denote the size of $n$th generation. If $m$ is the mean number of offspring per individual, then $Z_n:=X_n/m^n$ is a martingale.

(4) If $Z_n$ satisfies $E[Z_{n+1}\mid Z_1,...,Z_n,Y]=Z_n$ for some r.v. $Y$, then conditioning on $Z_1,...,Z_n$ and take expectations we have $E[Z_{n+1}\mid Z_1,...,Z_n]=Z_n$, so $Z_n$ is indeed a martingale.

(5) **Doob type martingale** Pick $X,Y_1,...,Y_n$ where $X\in L^1$. Let $Z_n=E[X\mid Y_1,...,Y_n]$. Then $E[Z_{n+1}\mid Y_1,...,Y_n]=Z_n$, which indicates that $Z_n$ is a martingale since $Z_1,...,Z_n\in \sigma(Y_1,...,Y_n)$.

(6) For a series of r.v.'s $X_i$, pick $Y_i=X_i-E[X_i\mid X_1,...,X_{i-1}]$. Let $Z_n=\sum_{i=1}^{n} Y_i$, then if $Z_i\in L^1$, $(Z_n)$ is a martingale with $EZ_n=0$. Also, $E[Z_{n+1}\mid X_1,...,X_n]=Z_n$. Since $Y_1,...,Y_n\in\sigma(X_1,...,X_n)$, this proves. Here, $Y_i$ can be seen as generated by "Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization" process.

### 6.2 Stopping times

**Definition** A r.v. $N\in Z_{>0}\cup\{\infty \}$ is a **random time** for the process $(Z_n)$ if $\{N=n \}\in \sigma(Z_1,...,Z_n)$. If $N<\infty$ a.s., we say $N$ is a **stopping time**.

**Definition** Let $N$ be a random time for the process $(Z_n)$, then $Z_{n\wedge N}$ is called a stopped process.

**Proposition 6.2.1** If $N$ is a random time for the martingale $Z_n$, then $Z_{n\wedge N}$ is also a martingale.

<font color='red'>_proof of Proposition 6.2.1_</font> Let $1_n=1_{n\le N}$, then $Z_{n\wedge N}=Z_{(n-1)\wedge N}+1_n (Z_n-Z_{n-1})$. 

Notice $1_n\in \sigma(Z_1,...,Z_{n-1})$, so by the martingale property of $Z_n$, we have 
$$
E[Z_{n\wedge N}\mid Z_1,...,Z_{n-1}]=E[Z_{(n-1)\wedge N}\mid Z_1,...,Z_{n-1}]+E[1_n (Z_n-Z_{n-1})\mid Z_1,...,Z_{n-1}]=Z_{(n-1)\wedge N}
$$
Moreover, $Z_{1\wedge N},...,Z_{n\wedge N}\in \sigma(Z_1,...,Z_n)$, so $Z_{n\wedge N}$ is a martingale. 

**Theorem 6.2.2 The optional stopping theorem** For a martingale $(Z_n)$ and a stopping time $N$, if one of the following conditions holds,

(a) $Z_{n\wedge N}$ are uniformly bounded

(b) $N$ is bounded

(c) $N\in L^1$, and $\exists M<\infty$ s.t. $E[|Z_{n+1}-Z_n|\mid Z_1,...,Z_n]<M$

Then $E[Z_{n\wedge N}]\to EZ_N$. So $EZ_N=EZ_1$.

_Remark_ Actually $Z_{n\wedge N}\to_{n\to\infty} Z_N$ a.s.. But for convergence of expectation, some regular conditions are needed.

_Remark_ The theorem states that, in a fair game, if a gambler uses a stopping strategy, then if the conditions in **Theorem 6.2.2** hold, he will result in the same fortune rather than earn/loss more.

**Corollary 6.2.3 Wald's Equation** If $X_i\in L^1$ i.i.d. and $N\in L^1$ is a stopping time, then $E\sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i=ENEX$.

<font color='red'>_proof of Corollary 6.2.3_</font> Pick $Z_n=\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i-EX_i$. The condition (c) in **Theorem 6.2.2** holds. So $EZ_N=EZ_1$. But $EZ_N=E\sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i-EXEN$ and $EZ_1=0$.

**Example 6.2(A)** Let's consider again the pattern matching problem.

_Setting_ Given $X_i\in Z_{>0}$ i.i.d., denote the time until a specific pattern occurs by $N$. We wonder $EN$. For a concrete example, $P(X=0)=1/2,P(X=1)=1/3,P(X=2)=1/6$, and we expect $020$.

_Solution_ The goal is to construct a martingale. 

- Imagine there is a casino. For a gambler, suppose at the beginning he has $1$ unit. The first day, he bet all on state $0$. If correct, he will receive $1/P(0)=2$, otherwise he will lose all and stop betting. The second day, if he has money, he bet all on state $2$. If correct, he will receive $2/P(2)=12$, otherwise he will lose all and stop betting. The third day, if he has money, he bet all on state $0$. If correct, he will receive $12/P(0)=24$, otherwise he will lose all, and whatever he stops betting. One can verify this process constructs a martingale.
- Since we don't know which day will be the beginning day for $020$ to occur, we start a gambler everyday. Let $X_n$ be the total winnings of casino at day $n$, then $X_n$ is a martingale with mean $0$ since for every gambler the game is fair.
- Set $N$ to be the first time when $020$ occurs, then at day $N$, totally there are $N$ units, and the gambler joins at day $N-2$ has $24$, the gambler joins at day $N$ has $2$, so the casino collects $N-26$.
- By the optional stopping theorem, $EX_N=EX_0$, i.e., $EN-26=0$. 

**Example 6.2(B)** Consider a 1-d RW starting from $0$. For each step it moves right with $p$ and left with $1-p$. Assume $p>1/2$. Fix $i$, consider the mean steps until it reaches $i$.

_Solution_ Let $X_i$ i.i.d. with $P(X_i=1)=1-P(X_i=-1)=p$, then the position after $n$ steps is $S_n$. Let $N=\min_n\{ S_n=i\}$. Then $S_N=i$. By Wald's equation, $ES_N=EX_iEN$, i.e., $i=(2p-1)EN$.

**Example 6.2(C)** 

_Setting_ Players $X,Y,Z$ has $x,y,z$ coins initially. At each play, a player is uniformly chosen to give 1 coin to another player uniformly chosen from the rest. When someone loses all, he quit, until there is only one player with $x+y+z$ coins. Find the expected number of plays for an epoch.

_Solution_ Denote $X_n,Y_n,Z_n$ to be the fortune of $X,Y,Z$. Denote the stopping time by $T$. For technical reason, continue the process in the way that it's allowed for the last two players to continue even their fortune is negative.

Let $M_n=X_nY_n+X_nZ_n+Y_nZ_n+n$, now we prove it's a martingale. Of course, we consider the $\sigma$-field generated by $X_i,Y_i,Z_i,i\le n$. Actually $(X_n,Y_n,Z_n)$ is a Markov chain. 

1. When $X_n,Y_n,Z_n>0$, we have 
   $$
   E[X_{n+1}Y_{n+1}\mid X_n=x,Y_n=y]=\frac{1}{6}((x-1)(y+1)+(x-1)y+(x+1)(y-1)+x(y-1)+(x+1)y+x(y+1))=xy-1/3
   $$
   So by Markovian property,
   $$
   E[M_{n+1}\mid X_i,Y_i,Z_i,i\le n]=E[M_{n+1}\mid X_n,Y_n,Z_n]=M_n
   $$

2. When $X_n=0$, we have 
   $$
   E[Y_nZ_n\mid X_n=0,Y_n=y,Z_n=z]=\frac{1}{2}((y+1)(z-1)+(y-1)(z+1))=yz-1
   $$
   So similarly 
   $$
   E[M_{n+1}\mid X_i,Y_i,Z_i,i\le n]=E[M_{n+1}\mid X_n,Y_n,Z_n]=M_n
   $$

So by optional stopping theorem, 
$$
EM_T=EM_0=xy+xz+yz
$$
$M_T=T$ since two of $X_n,Y_n,Z_n$ are $0$. So $EM_T=xy+xz+yz$.

**Example 6.2(D)** 

_Setting_ There are $n$ hats of $n$ people in a pile. Each time everyone pick a hat uniformly, and if one found his hat he will leave, if not he will put the hat on the pile and continue this process. Let $R$ denote the number of rounds until all people have a match, we wonder $ER$.

_Solution_ Define $X_i$ to be the number of matches of each epoch. For $i>R$, define $X_i=1$. 

> [!Tip]
>
> Recall that for matching problem, at each epoch the mean number of matches is $1$.

Define $Z_k=\sum_{i=1}^{k}(X_i-E[X_i\mid X_1,...,X_{i-1}])$. We have proven $Z_k$ is a martingale. Moreover, $E[X_i\mid X_1,...,X_{i-1}]=1$: if $\sum_{j=1}^{i-1} X_i<n$, this follows from the recall; if $\sum_{j=1}^{i-1} X_i\ge n$, this follows from our definition. So $Z_k=\sum_{i=1}^{k} X_i-1$. 

By optional stopping theorem,
$$
0=EZ_R=E\sum_{i=1}^{R} X_i-ER=n-ER
$$

### 6.3 Azuma's Inequality for martingales

In situations where these r.v.'s don't change too rapidly, Azuma's inequality enables us to obtain useful bounds on their probabilities.

**Theorem 6.3.3 Azuma's inequality** Let $Z_n$ be a martingale with $EZ_n=\mu$. Let $Z_0=\mu$ and suppose that $\exists \alpha_i,\beta_i\ge 0: -\alpha_i\le Z_i-Z_{i-1}\le \beta_i$. Then $\forall n\ge 0,a>0$, $P(Z_n-\mu\ge a), P(Z_n-\mu\le -a)\le \exp(-2a^2/\sum_{i=1}^{n} (\alpha_i+\beta_i)^2)$.

> **Lemma 6.3.1** Suppose $X\in[-\alpha,\beta]$ and $EX=0$, then for any convex $f$, $Ef(X)\le \frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta}f(-\alpha)+\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}f(\beta)$.
>
> **Lemma 6.3.2** $\theta e^{(1-\theta)x}+(1-\theta)e^{-\theta x}\le e^{x^2/8}$.

<font color='red'>_proof of Theorem 6.3.3_</font>

WLOG set $\mu=0$ and prove one side.

1. By Markov inequality, $P(Z_n\ge a)\le [E\exp(cZ_n)]e^{-ca}$.

2. Let $W_n=\exp(cZ_n)$, by **Lemma 6.3.1** and **Lemma 6.3.2**,
   $$
   E[W_n\mid Z_{n-1}]=\exp(cZ_{n-1})E[\exp(c(Z_n-Z_{n-1}))\mid Z_{n-1}]\le W_{n-1}[\frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n+\beta_n}\exp(-c\alpha_n)+\frac{\alpha_n}{\alpha_n+\beta_n}\exp(c\beta_n)]\le W_{n-1}\exp(c^2(\alpha_n+\beta_n)^2/8)
   $$
   Taking expectation yields
   $$
   EW_n\le EW_{n-1}\exp(c^2(\alpha_n+\beta_n)^2/8)
   $$
   And iterating this yields
   $$
   EW_n\le \exp(-ca+\frac{c^2}{8}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\alpha_i+\beta_i)^2 )
   $$
   Pick $c=4a\bigg/\sum_{i=1}^{n} (\alpha_i+\beta_i)^2$, we will reach our conclusion.

**Example 6.3(A)** Let $X_i$ be r.v.'s s.t. $EX_1=0$ and $E[X_i\mid X_1,...,X_{i-1}]=0$. Then $S_n$ is a martingale. If $X_i\in [-\alpha_i,\beta_i]$, we have 
$$
P(S_n\ge a)\le \exp(-2a^2\bigg/\sum_{i=1}^{n} (\alpha_i+\beta_i)^2)
$$
Azuma's inequality is often applied in conjunction with a Doob type martingale.

**Example 6.3(B)** Suppose $n$ balls are put into $m$ urns in the manner that each ball independently uniformly chooses a urn. Let $X$ denote the number of empty urns. Now we case about the tail distribution of $X$.

- Let $A_i$ be the event that the $n$th urn is empty, then $X=\sum_{i=1}^{m} 1_{A_i}$, and 
  $$
  EX=\sum_{i=1}^{m} E1_{A_i}=\sum_{i=1}^{m} (1-1/m)^n=m(1-1/m)^n:=\mu
  $$

- Let $X_j$ denote the urn in which the $j$th ball is placed. Let $Z_0=EX$ and $Z_i=E[X\mid X_1,...,X_i]$. Notice $Z_i$ is a martingale and $X=Z_n$.

- Now determine the difference of $Z_i$. 

  - First, actually the urn of the first ball doesn't give any information about the number of empty urn, i.e., $E[X\mid X_1]=E[X]$. Or one can directly compute to verify.

  - For $i\ge 2$, Let $D$ denote the nonempty urns after putting $i-1$ balls. We have 
    $$
    E[X\mid X_1,...,X_{i-1}]=(m-D)(1-1/m)^{n-i+1}
    $$
    And 
    $$
    E[X\mid X_1,...,X_i]=\begin{cases}(m-D)(1-1/m)^{n-i}&\text{if }X_i=X_j\exists j<i\\(m-D-1)(1-1/m)^{n-i}&\text{if }X_i\ne X_j\forall j<i   \end{cases}
    $$
    So 
    $$
    Z_i-Z_{i-1}=\begin{cases}(m-D)(1-1/m)^{n-i}/m&\text{if }X_i=X_j\exists j<i\\-D(1-1/m)^{n-i}/m&\text{if }X_i\ne X_j\forall j<i   \end{cases}
    $$
    So $Z_i-Z_{i-1}\in[-\alpha_i,\beta_i]$, where $\alpha_i=\min (\frac{i-1}{m},1)(1-1/m)^{n-i}$ and $\beta_i=(1-1/m)^{n-i+1}$.

- Use Azuma's inequality we can get 
  $$
  P(X-\mu\ge a)\le \exp(-2a^2\bigg/\sum_{i=2}^n (\alpha_i+\beta_i)^2)
  $$

Azuma's inequality is often applied to a Doob type martingale for which $|Z_i-Z_{i-1}|\le 1$. For example, 

**Corollary 6.3.4** Let $h$ be such function: If $\vec x$ and $\vec y$ differs at most $1$ coordinate, then $|h(\vec x)-h(\vec y)|\le 1$. Let $X_1,...,X_n$ be independent r.v.'s, and $\vec X=(X_1,...,X_n)$. We have:
$$
P(h(\vec X)-E[h(\vec X)]\ge a)\le e^{-a^2/2n}
$$
<font color='red'>_proof of Corollary 6.3.4_</font> Notice $Z_i=E[h(\vec X)\mid X_1,...,X_i]$ is a martingale and $Z_n=h(\vec X)$. Moreover, $|Z_i-Z_{i-1}|\le 1$. Then use Azuma's inequality.
