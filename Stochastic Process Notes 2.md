#### 4. Brownian motion

***Definition*** A standard Brownian Motion is a probability measure on $C^0(\mathbb R_+,\mathbb R)$ s.t. paths $(B_t)_{t\geq 0}$ satisfy:

(i) $B_0=0$ a.s.

(ii) (stationary increments) $\forall t,s\geq 0$, $B_{t+s}-B_t\sim\mathcal N(0,t-s)$.

(iii) (independent increments) $\forall t_1<s_1<t_2<s_2$, $B_{s_2}-B_{s_1}$ is independent of $B_{s_1}-B_{t_1}$.

Naturally we care about its existence. Now we give a claim:

**Theorem** standard Brownian motion exists and is unique. 

First we construct a random function $\omega\rightarrow B(\cdot,\omega)$, where $B(t,\omega)$ is a path satisfying above definitions. Refer [Paul Lévy’s construction of Brownian motion — Proswit](https://www.proswit.com/levy_construction) to the construction of Levy. The strategy is to couple r.w. on dyadic numbers and pass to the limit. Since the limit is a.s. cts, the process is determined in law by its restrictions on dyadic numbers.

Now we want to construct it as the unique prob. measure on $C^0(\mathbb R_+,\mathbb R)$ satisfying above definitions. The Borel $\sigma$-algebra is generated by open balls $\mathbb B(f,\epsilon)=\{u\in C^0(\mathbb R_+,\mathbb R: \sup_t |f(t)-u(t)|<\epsilon\}$, and we have $\{W\in\overline{\mathbb B(f,\epsilon)}\}=\cap_{q\in \mathbb Q_+} \{|W_q-f(q)|\leq \epsilon\}$. So the collection of $\{|W_q-f(q)|\leq \epsilon\}$ generate the $\sigma$-algebra, for $q\in\mathbb Q,\epsilon\in\mathbb R_+, f\in C^0(\mathbb R_+,\mathbb R)$. This implies that a Borel measure on this space is characterized by the finite dimensional distribution, i.e., the family of laws $(B_{t_1},...,B_{t_n}), n\in\mathbb N, t_i \in \mathbb Q_+$. The definition of SBW implies that those finite dimensional distributions are Gaussian variables with a prescribed cov.. So it's unique.

_Remark_ We get an alternative characterization of BM as the unique cts, centered Gaussian process $(B_t)_{t\geq 0}$ with cov $\mathbb E[B_t B_s]=\min(t,s)$.

**Definition**  **Brownian motion on $\mathbb R^d$ **, $d\geq 1$ is defined as d independent BM on $\mathbb R$, $(B_t^1,...,B_t^d)$.

What is the role of the random walk $B_m$ w.r.t. BM? We can look at the $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal F_m:=\sigma(B_{k 2^{-m}},0\leq k<2^m)$. Consider space $\mathcal L_n=\{\text{piecewise affine cts functions on [0,1], affine in each }[k,k+1)2^{-m}\}$ Then $B_m$ is the orthogonal projection of BM on this space, so it's the "best approximation".

**Properties of BM**

**The Brownian scale relation** For all $\sigma>0$, $(B_{\sigma^2 t})=_d (\sigma B_t)$.

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> Use the second characterization. This hints BM will not be differentiable.

**Proposition** Let $U$ be a $d\times d$ orthogonal matrix and $(B_t^1,...,B_t^d)$ a d-dim BM, then $(B_t^1,...,B_t^d)U=_d (B_t^1,...,B_t^d)$

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> They are Gaussian process with same cov.

**Regular properties**

**Theorem** Let $\gamma>0$. BM is globally $\gamma$-Holder a.s. iff $\gamma<1/2$. (So BM is not differentiable.)

We need:

**Theorem Kolmogorov's Criterion** Let $(X_t)_{0\leq t\leq 1}$ be a cts process in [0,1] s.t. $\mathbb E[|X_t-X_s|^\beta]\leq K|t-s|^{1+\alpha}$ for some $\alpha,\beta,K>0$. Then a.s. exists $C(\omega)>0$ s.t. $|X_t-X_s|\leq C(\omega)|t-s|^\gamma$ for all $\gamma<\alpha/\beta$ and all $t,s\in[0,1]$. 

_Note_ The constant is random but a.s. finite

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> Let $\Delta_n=\{k2^{-n}:0\leq k\leq 2^n\}$ and $M_n=\max\{|X_{(k+1)2^{-n}}-X_{k2^{-n}}|\}$. By assumption, for all $\beta>0$, we have $\mathbb E[M_n^\beta]\leq \mathbb E[\sum_{n=0}^{2^m-1}|X_{(k+1)2^{-n}}-X_{k2^{-n}}|^\beta]\leq K2^m 2^{-m(1+\alpha)}=K2^{-\alpha m}$, now for $\gamma<\alpha/\beta$, let $M^\gamma:=\sum_{n=0}^\infty 2^{n\gamma}M_n$, and $\|M^\gamma\|_{L^\beta}\leq K^{1/\beta}\sum_{n=0}^\infty 2^{n(\gamma-\alpha/\beta)}<\infty$.

Now let $t<s,t,s\in[0,1]$ and $m$ s.t. $2^{-(m+1)}<|t-s|\leq2^{-m}$. We can cover the interval $[s,t]$ by dyadic intervals in $(\Delta_n)_{n\geq m}$ such that at most two intervals are in $\Delta_n$ for each $n>m$. Therefore, 
$$
|X_t-X_s|\leq 2\sum_{n=m}^\infty M_n=2\sum_{n=m}^\infty 2^{-n\gamma}(2^{n\gamma}M_n)\leq 2\cdot 2^{-m\gamma}\sum_{n=m}^\infty 2^{n\gamma}M_n\leq 2^{1-m\gamma}M^\gamma
\\\leq 2^{1+\gamma}M^\gamma|t-s|^\gamma
$$
Take $C(\omega)=2^{1+\gamma}M^\gamma$, it's bounded in $L^\beta$.

return to the proof of Holder regularity.

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> 

Suppose $\gamma<1/2$. By transition and scaling, $\mathbb E|B_t-B_s|^\beta=(t-s)^{\beta/2}\mathbb E|B_1|^\beta<\infty$. By Kolmogorov's Criterion, Let $\alpha+1=\beta/2$, we have BM is $\gamma$-Holder for $\gamma<\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{\beta}$, and since $\beta$ is arbitrary large, this can be true for all $\gamma<1/2$.

Let $\gamma\geq 1/2$, we are going to prove that a.s. BM is not $\gamma$-Holder cts. 

Let $t\in[0,1]$ and consider the increments $(B_{t+2^{-m}}-B_{t+2^{-m-1}})_{m\geq 0}:=(\xi_m)$, which are independent variables with var $2^{-m-1}$. Hence, $\limsup_{m\rightarrow\infty}2^{(m+1)/2}\xi_m=\infty$ since the term is i.i.d. and we can use Borel-Cantelli lemma.

On the other hand, $|\xi_m|\leq |B_{t+2^{-m}}-B_t|+|B_{t+2^{-m-1}}-B_t|$. It follows that $\limsup |B_{t+2^{-m}}-B_t|2^{m/2}=\infty$. Therefore $B_s$ is not 1/2-Holder cts at t. We have proved for all $t\in[0,1]$, BM is not $1/2$-Holder at t a.s., hence a.s. for all $t\in[0,1]\cap\mathbb Q$, it's not $1/2$-Holder at t (since the union of countable zero measure sets are of measure 0 but may be wrong for uncountable case). 

This is enough to say $\mathbb P(\text{B is globally 1/2 Holder on [0,1]})=0$. But we cannot say "By continuity, it's not 1/2-Holder for all t". Actually, we have a.s. there exists an $1/2$-Holder point.

**Markov Property** 

Transition probability: Let $0<s<t$. By definition, for all cts bounded function $F$ on $\mathbb R$, 
$$
\mathbb E[F(B_{t+s})|\mathcal F_t]=\mathbb E[F(B_t+B_{t+s}-B_t)|\mathcal F_t]=\int_\mathbb R \mathbb E[F(B_t+y)]\frac{e^{-y^2/2s}}{\sqrt{2\pi s}}dy= P_s\mathbb E[F(B_t)]
$$
where $P_s:C^0(\mathbb R)\rightarrow C^0(\mathbb R)$ is defined by $P_tf(x)=\int f(x+y)p_t(y)dy$ is a transition on prob. we have seen before. And $P_t(x,dy)=\frac{e^{-(x-y)^2/2t}}{\sqrt{2\pi t}}$ is the transition prob.. This gives us the Markov property. And $P_{t+s}=P_t\circ P_s$ . This consists of a semigroup of transitions.

**Martingales**

Given $(\Omega,(\mathcal F_t)_{t\geq 0},\mathbb P)$ a filtered space and $\mu_x$ defined on $C^0(\mathbb R_+)$ is a BM starting from x.

**Definition** Say $(X_t)$ is an  **$(\mathcal F_t)_{t\geq 0}$ Brownian motion** if it's an adapted cts process and $\forall t,s\geq 0$, $X_{t+s}-X_t\sim\mathcal N(0,s)$ independent of $\mathcal F_t$.

There are some examples of martingales of $(\mathcal F_t)$-BM.

**Example** $X_t$: $\forall t,s\geq 0$, $\mathbb E[X_{t+s}|\mathcal F_t]=\mathbb E[X_{t+s}-X_t+X_t|\mathcal F_t]=X_t$.

**Example** Recall 

> [!NOTE]
>
> when $X_t$ is a continuous local martingale, $X_t$ has finite quadratic variation, denoted by $[X_t]$. Moreover, $[X_t]$ is the unique increasing, adapted process s.t. $X_t^2-[X_t]$ is a cts local martingale.

And we have: $\mathbb E[X_{t+s}^2|\mathcal F_t]=\mathbb E[(X_{t+s}-X_t+X_t)^2|\mathcal F_t]=X_t^2+s$, so $(X_t^2-t)$ is a martingale. Moreover, $[B_t]=t$.

_Remark_ The converse is true. This just needs some stochastic calculus, 

**Example** (Exponential martingale) For $a\in\mathbb C$, Let $Z_a(t)=\exp(aX_t-\frac{a^2}{2}t)$, then $Z_a(t)$ is a martingale:
$$
\mathbb E[\exp (aX_{t+s}-\frac{a^2}{2}(t+s))|\mathcal F_t]=Z_a(t)\mathbb E[\exp(a(X_{t+s}-X_t)-\frac{a^2}{2}s)|\mathcal F_t]=Z_a(t)
$$
The expectation is just the of char. function valued at -i.

**Example** Hermite polynomials

> [!NOTE]
>
> **Definition** **Hermite polynomial** $H_n(x)=(-1)^ne^{x^2/2}\partial_x^ne^{-x^2/2}$. Another form is $H_n(x)=(x-\partial_x)^n\mathbb 1$. 
>
> Its generating series is $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{a^n}{n!}H_n(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-a)^n}{n!}e^{x^2/2}\partial_x^ne^{-x^2/2}=e^{ax-a^2/2}$.

Define $M_n(t)=t^{n/2}H_n(\frac{X_t}{\sqrt{t}})$. We have $M_n(t)=\partial_a^n Z_a(t)$ from the generating series, and since $Z_a(t)$ is holomorphic as for a, we have $M_n(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\oint Z_a(t)\frac{da}{a^n}$.

By Fubini theorem, $\mathbb E(M_n(t+s)|\mathcal F_t)=M_n(t)$, so it's a martingale.

Next, we consider BM on $\mathbb R^d$.

**Theorem** Let $(X_t)_{t\geq 0}$ be an $(\mathcal F_t)_{t\geq 0}$ BM. Let $f\in C^2(\mathbb R^d)$ and define $M_t=f(X_t)-f(X_0)-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^t\Delta f(X_s)ds$. Then $M_t$ is a cts $\mathcal F_t$ martingale.

_Remark_ Suppose f is harmonic in some nonempty open area $D\subset \mathbb R^d$, and let $\tau=\inf\{t\geq 0, X_t\notin D \}$, then by this theorem $f(X_{t\wedge\tau})$ is a martingale.

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> First $M_t$ is adapted and integrable.

By definition, 
$$
\mathbb E[M_t]=\int_{C^0(\mathbb R_+,\mathbb R^d)}f(W_t)-f(W_0)-\int_0^t\frac{1}{2}\Delta f(W_s)ds\ d\mu_x(W)
$$
For all $s\in[0,t]$, by the transition prob.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb E[M_t-M_s]=&\mathbb E[f(W_t)-f(W_s)-\int_s^t\frac{1}{2}\Delta f(X_\tau)d\tau]
\\=&\int_{\mathbb R^d}f(y)p_t(x,y)-\int_{\mathbb R^d}f(y)p_s(x,y)-\frac{1}{2}\int_s^t \int_{\mathbb R^d}p_\tau(x,y)\Delta f(y)dyd\tau
\end{aligned}
$$
where $p_t(x,y)$ denotes the density at $x-y$ of $\mathcal N(0,t)$.

Since $p_t(x,y)$ is the heat kernel, we have $\Delta p_t=2\partial_t p_t$, so by integration by parts twice, we finally conclude
$$
\int_s^t \int_{\mathbb R^d}p_\tau(x,y)\Delta f(y)dyd\tau=\int_s^t \int_{\mathbb R^d}f(y)\Delta p_\tau(x,y)dyd\tau=\int_s^t \int_{\mathbb R^d}f(y)\partial_t p_\tau(x,y)dyd\tau=2\int_{\mathbb R^d}f(y)p_t(x,y)-2\int_{\mathbb R^d}f(y)p_s(x,y)
$$
So $\mathbb E[M_t]=\mathbb E[M_s]$. 

Let $s\rightarrow 0$ and recall $M_0=0 a.s.$, by bounded convergence theorem $\mathbb E[M_t]=\mathbb E[M_0]=0$.

Finally we just need to verify the definition of martingale, which is trivial given above argument.

**Theorem Strong Markov Property** Let $(X_t)$ be a $(\mathcal F_t)$ BM and $T$ be an a.s. finite stopping time, then $(X_{T+t}-X_T)$ is an $(\mathcal F_{T+t})$ BM.

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> Clearly it's an adapted cts process. To verify it's Gaussian, use char. function. 

Below are some further properties.

**Proposition Reflection principle** Let $(X_t)_{t\geq 0}$ be a BM starting from 0, and $a\in\mathbb R$, $T=\inf\{ t\geq 0, X_t=a\}$, and $Z_t$ is defined that when $t\geq T$, it's $Z_a-X_t$, otherwise i's just $X_t$. Then $Z_t$ is also a BM starting from 0 with same distribution.

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> By strong Markov property, conditionally $T<\infty$, set $\tilde X_t=X_{T+t}-X_T$, then $\tilde X_t$ is a $\mathcal F_{T+t}$ BM starting from 0 and independent of $\mathcal F_T$. The same is true for $-\tilde X_t$. So $X_t=X_{t\wedge T}+\tilde X_{(t-T)^+}\mathbb 1_{T<\infty}$ and $Z_t=X_{t\wedge T}-\tilde X_{(t-T)^+}\mathbb 1_{T<\infty}$ have same distribution.

**Corollary** Under the same hypothesis, if $T<\infty$ a.s., we have $\mathbb P(T\leq t)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}}\int_a^\infty \exp(-\frac{x^2}{2t})dx$.

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> 
$$
\mathbb P(T<t)=\mathbb P(X_t>a\ or\ Z_t>a)=2\mathbb P(X_t>a)=2\int_a^\infty\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi t}}\exp(-\frac{x^2}{2t})dx
$$
Moreover, as $t\rightarrow\infty$, the probability has limit 1.

**Proposition** Let $(X_t)$ be a BM starting from 0 and $a,b>0$, $T_a$ is the first time touching a. Then (i) $\mathbb P(T_{-a}<T_b)=\frac{b}{a+b}$ (ii) $\mathbb E[\min(T_{-a}, T_b)]=ab$

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> (i) Apply optional stopping theorem and push t to infinity,
$$
0=\mathbb E[X_{t\wedge T_{-a}\wedge T_b}]=-a\mathbb P(T_{-a}<T_b)+b(1-\mathbb P(T_{-a}<T_b))
$$
(ii) similarly,
$$
0=\mathbb E[X_{t\wedge T_{-a}\wedge T_b}^2-t\wedge T_{-a}\wedge T_b]=a^2\mathbb P(T_{-a}<T_b)+b^2(1-\mathbb P(T_{-a}<T_b))-\mathbb E[T_{-a}-T_b]=ab-\mathbb E[T_{-a}\wedge T_b]
$$
**Recurrence and transience**

**Theorem** Let $(X_t)$ be a BM on $\mathbb R^d$, 

(i) d=1, $\mathbb P(\{t\geq 0, X_t=0\}\text{ is unbounded})=1$

(ii) d=2, $\mathbb P(X_t=0\ \exists t>0)=0$, but $\forall\epsilon$, $\mathbb P(\{t\geq 0, |X_t|\leq \epsilon\}\text{ is unbounded})=1$

(iii) d=3, $\mathbb P(|X_t|\rightarrow\infty)=1$.

<font color='red'>_proof_</font> 

(i) Define $T_n=\inf\{t>n: X_t=0\}$. 

By reflection principle, $\mathbb P(\sup_{0<s\leq t}X_s>a)=2\mathbb P(X_t>a)\rightarrow 1$, as $a\rightarrow 0$, and the same argument shows $\mathbb P(\inf_{0<s\leq t}X_s<-a)=2\mathbb P(-X_t<-a)\rightarrow 1$. This implies $X_t$ must have hit 0 by continuity for all $t$. So $T_0<\infty$ a.s..

By Markov property, any $T_n<\infty$ a.s. This indicates (i).

(ii) Set $p_0(x)=\mathbb P(X_t=0\ \exists\ t>0)$ and for $\epsilon>0$, $p_\epsilon(x)=\mathbb P(|X_t|<\epsilon\ \exists\ t>0)$.

Let $0<a<b$ and fix a function in $C^2(\mathbb R^2)$ s.t. $f(x)=\ln(|x|)$ in a neighborhood of annulus $\{a<R<b\}$, we have $\Delta f=0$.

By previous thm, we have $M_t=f(X_t)-f(X_0)-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^t \Delta f(X_s)ds$ is a martingale. 

Fix the stopping time $T_a=\inf\{t\geq 0, |X_t|=a \}$ and $T=T_a\wedge T_b$. We know $T<\infty$ a.s..

By optional stopping theorem, for starting point $X_0=x$ with $a<|x|<b$, 
$$
\mathbb E[M_T]=\ln a\mathbb P(T_a<T_b)+\ln b(1-\mathbb P(T_a<T_b))=\mathbb E[M_0]=\ln |x|
$$
So
$$
\mathbb P(T_a<T_b)=\frac{\ln |x|-\ln b}{\ln a-\ln b}\rightarrow 0(a\rightarrow 0)
$$
So the prob. of twice visit to 0 is 0.
